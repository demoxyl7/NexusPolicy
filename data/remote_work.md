## 1. Purpose and Philosophy

The organization recognizes that flexible work arrangements can enhance productivity, support employee well-being, and broaden access to talent. This Remote Work Policy establishes the standards, expectations, and boundaries that govern all hybrid and fully remote work arrangements. It applies to any employee who performs work outside of a company-designated office location — whether occasionally, on a scheduled hybrid basis, or full-time.

Remote work is a privilege, not an entitlement. Eligibility is determined by role requirements, performance standing, and manager approval. Not all roles are suitable for remote work, particularly those requiring physical presence, hands-on equipment access, or real-time in-person collaboration. This policy is not a guarantee of remote work availability; it defines the conditions under which it may be offered and maintained.

## 2. Scope: Hybrid vs. Fully Remote

### 2.1 Hybrid Work Arrangements

Hybrid employees split their work time between a company office and a remote location, typically their home. The company's default hybrid model requires a minimum of two in-office days per week, with specific days determined by team and department leadership. Hybrid schedules must be agreed upon in writing between the employee and their manager and documented in HR systems before taking effect.

Hybrid employees are expected to treat both their in-office and remote days with equal professionalism. Attendance at in-office days is mandatory unless the employee is traveling for approved business purposes, on approved leave, or has received an explicit exemption from their manager.

Teams are encouraged to coordinate in-office days so that collaborative work can occur face-to-face. Individual hybrid schedules that never overlap with teammates' in-office days undermine the purpose of the hybrid model and may be subject to revision at management's discretion.

### 2.2 Fully Remote Arrangements

Fully remote employees work exclusively from a location outside company premises on a permanent or long-term basis. These arrangements require VP-level approval in addition to manager approval, and must be reviewed and re-authorized annually. Fully remote employees are still expected to travel to company offices for onboarding, annual kick-offs, team off-sites, and any other events designated as in-person mandatory by leadership.

Travel costs for mandatory in-person events are covered by the company under the standard Travel and Expense Policy. Fully remote employees who repeatedly decline mandatory in-person attendance without valid justification may have their remote arrangements revoked.

### 2.3 Temporary Remote Work

Employees may request temporary remote work (up to four consecutive weeks) due to personal circumstances — such as family care needs, housing transitions, or short-term health situations — without requiring a formal remote work agreement. Requests must be submitted to and approved by the direct manager. Extensions beyond four weeks require HR involvement and a formal arrangement.

## 3. Home Office Equipment and Safety Standards

### 3.1 Company-Provided Equipment

The company provides every remote and hybrid employee with the standard equipment package described in the IT Onboarding section of the Onboarding Guide. This includes a company-issued laptop, required peripherals, and access to all necessary software. Employees must use company-issued equipment for all work activities. Use of personal laptops or devices for substantive work tasks is prohibited unless explicitly approved by IT for a specific, documented reason.

### 3.2 Home Office Ergonomics and Safety

Employees are responsible for maintaining a safe, ergonomically appropriate workspace. The company is not liable for injuries sustained while working from home that result from unsafe or non-compliant home office setups. However, the organization provides the following support to help employees meet safety standards:

Employees may submit a request for an ergonomic assessment through the HR portal. A certified ergonomic specialist (contracted by HR) will conduct a virtual assessment and recommend adjustments or equipment. Employees are also eligible for the annual Home Office Allowance of $300, which may be used for ergonomic equipment such as monitor stands, keyboard trays, lumbar support cushions, or wrist rests. This allowance resets each January 1 and does not carry over.

Required home office standards include: a dedicated workspace that allows for focused work free from significant interruption during work hours; sufficient ambient lighting to prevent eye strain; a stable, level surface for the computer; adequate ventilation; and a chair that supports upright posture. Working from beds, vehicles, or other non-standard surfaces is discouraged and, for video meetings with external parties, prohibited.

### 3.3 Equipment Care and Return

Company equipment taken off-site remains the property of the organization. Employees must take reasonable precautions to protect equipment from damage, theft, or loss. Any damage must be reported to IT immediately. Equipment must be returned in good condition within five business days of employment termination, a role change that eliminates remote eligibility, or upon the company's request.

## 4. Mandatory Core Hours

### 4.1 Core Hours Defined

To facilitate collaboration across time zones and maintain operational cohesion, all remote and hybrid employees — regardless of their personal work schedule — are required to be available, responsive, and actively working during Core Hours: **10:00 AM to 3:00 PM Eastern Time (ET), Monday through Friday**, excluding company-designated holidays.

"Available" means: responsive to messages within 30 minutes, able to join a call on reasonable short notice, and not engaged in personal activities that would prevent participation in work discussions.

### 4.2 Scheduling Within Core Hours

Core Hours represent a floor, not a ceiling. Employees are still expected to fulfill their full weekly hours and complete their work responsibilities. How employees structure their time outside Core Hours — whether they work early, late, or in split shifts — is generally flexible, subject to manager approval and business needs.

Recurring meetings should be scheduled during Core Hours wherever possible to maximize attendance. Meetings scheduled outside Core Hours require advance manager approval if attendance is mandatory for remote employees in significantly different time zones.

### 4.3 Core Hours Exceptions

Employees may request a temporary Core Hours exception (up to two weeks) for circumstances such as medical appointments, family emergencies, or time-zone-based travel. Exceptions beyond two weeks require HR and manager co-approval and must be documented. Repeated Core Hours non-compliance without an approved exception is treated as a performance issue.

## 5. Internet Bandwidth Requirements

### 5.1 Minimum Speed Requirement

All remote employees are required to maintain a home internet connection with a minimum download speed of **100 Mbps** and a minimum upload speed of **20 Mbps**. These thresholds are necessary to support video conferencing, VPN connectivity, cloud-based collaboration tools, and file transfers without performance degradation.

Employees should test their internet speed at least quarterly using a reputable speed test tool (e.g., fast.com or speedtest.net) and retain screenshots. If speeds consistently fall below the minimum threshold, employees must notify IT immediately.

### 5.2 Internet Reimbursement

The company provides a monthly internet reimbursement of up to $60 for fully remote employees and up to $40 for hybrid employees, submitted through the standard expense reporting process with a copy of the monthly bill. Reimbursement is not available for employees living in company-provided housing.

### 5.3 Backup Connectivity

Employees in roles that require continuous availability (e.g., customer-facing roles, on-call engineers) are expected to maintain a backup internet option — such as a mobile hotspot — for use during primary internet outages. The company will reimburse up to $30/month for a dedicated mobile hotspot data plan for eligible employees in these roles, with manager pre-approval.

## 6. Security Protocols for Public Wi-Fi

Remote employees frequently work from coffee shops, libraries, airports, hotels, and other locations that offer public Wi-Fi. Public Wi-Fi networks are inherently insecure — they are frequently unencrypted and may be operated by malicious actors conducting man-in-the-middle attacks.

**When using any public Wi-Fi network, employees must:**

Connect to the company VPN immediately and maintain the VPN connection for the duration of the session. The company VPN encrypts all traffic between your device and company systems, shielding your activity from other parties on the network. Never use public Wi-Fi without VPN connectivity — no exceptions.

Do not conduct video meetings involving confidential client information, sensitive financial data, or regulated personal data from a public location where conversations may be overheard. Use headphones and position your screen to minimize visibility to nearby individuals.

Do not connect to networks named "Free Wi-Fi," "Airport Free Internet," or similar generic names without verifying with the venue that it is their legitimate network. Evil twin networks — rogue access points designed to mimic legitimate networks — are a common attack vector.

Report any suspected security incident (e.g., unexpected VPN disconnection during a sensitive session, unauthorized access attempts to your accounts) to the IT Security team immediately at security@company.com.

## 7. Dependent Care Limitations During Work Hours

### 7.1 Policy on Dependent Care

Remote work is not a substitute for dependent care. Employees with children, elderly family members, or others who require active supervision and care during the day are expected to make appropriate dependent care arrangements for their standard work hours. The company recognizes that occasional, brief interruptions are a normal part of home life — a child needing a snack or a quick check-in on a family member is not a policy violation.

However, employees cannot be the primary active caregiver for a dependent who requires consistent attention during Core Hours. Working while simultaneously providing primary childcare for young children is not sustainable for the employee and creates performance, availability, and professionalism risks for the team.

### 7.2 Available Support

Employees who are experiencing dependent care challenges are encouraged to speak confidentially with HR. Available resources include: the Employee Assistance Program (EAP), which can assist with identifying local childcare providers and eldercare referrals; the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) and applicable state leave laws for qualifying care situations; and the company's flexible scheduling options, which may allow shifts in work hours to accommodate non-overlapping care arrangements in some roles.

## 8. Tax Implications for Working from Different States or Countries

### 8.1 State Tax Obligations

Working remotely from a state different from the one listed in your employment agreement may create tax obligations — for both you and the company — in that state. Many states impose income tax withholding requirements on employers when an employee works within their borders, and several states have adopted aggressive "convenience of the employer" rules that may require additional employer-side filings.

Before working remotely from a state other than your state of record for more than 15 consecutive calendar days, you must notify HR and Legal in writing. The company will assess whether the arrangement creates nexus (a legal and tax obligation) in that state and what actions are required.

### 8.2 International Remote Work

Working from a foreign country — even for a short period — can trigger significant legal, tax, and immigration complications, including: personal income tax obligations in the foreign jurisdiction, employer tax withholding obligations, work permit and visa requirements, permanent establishment risk for the company, and complications with employment law protections. As a result, international remote work is generally not permitted without advance written approval from the Legal, Finance, and HR teams. Unapproved international remote work may result in disciplinary action and could expose both the employee and the company to substantial legal and financial liability.

Employees who hold dual citizenship or permanent residency in another country and wish to work from that country temporarily should initiate a formal request through HR at least 60 days in advance.

### 8.3 Responsibility for Personal Tax Compliance

The company will make reasonable efforts to withhold taxes in the appropriate jurisdiction. However, employees are ultimately responsible for their own personal tax compliance. The company recommends consulting a personal tax advisor if you intend to work remotely from a jurisdiction other than your primary work state for any extended period.
