## 1. Our Commitment to Professionalism

This Code of Conduct reflects the values that define how we work together and how we represent ourselves to the world. Every person who works for or with this organization — regardless of role, seniority, location, or employment type — is expected to act with integrity, respect, and good judgment at all times.

The standards in this document are not merely aspirational. They are expectations. Adherence to this Code is a condition of employment. Violations may result in disciplinary action, up to and including immediate termination, and may also carry civil or criminal consequences depending on the nature of the conduct.

We believe that a healthy, professional workplace is not just ethically right — it is also strategically essential. Organizations where employees feel respected, treated fairly, and empowered to raise concerns consistently outperform those where misconduct is tolerated or concealed. This Code exists to protect everyone: you, your colleagues, our clients, and the organization itself.

If you have questions about how this Code applies to a specific situation, contact Human Resources or the Legal & Compliance team. When in doubt, ask.

## 2. Anti-Harassment Policy

### 2.1 Defining Harassment

The organization is committed to providing a workplace free from harassment in all forms. Harassment is any unwelcome conduct based on a protected characteristic — including but not limited to race, color, religion, sex, gender identity, gender expression, sexual orientation, national origin, age, disability, marital status, pregnancy, or veteran status — that creates a hostile, intimidating, or offensive work environment, or that results in an adverse employment action.

Harassment can take many forms. It may be overt or subtle, physical or verbal, in-person or digital. Examples include:

**Verbal Harassment:**
- Slurs, epithets, derogatory comments, or jokes targeting a person's protected characteristic
- Unwelcome comments about a person's body, appearance, clothing, or personal life
- Persistent, unwanted romantic or sexual advances
- Threats, intimidation, or verbal abuse

**Physical Harassment:**
- Unwanted physical contact, including touching, hugging, or grabbing
- Blocking someone's movement or physically intimidating them
- Any form of assault or battery

**Visual or Written Harassment:**
- Displaying or distributing offensive images, cartoons, posters, or materials
- Sending harassing messages via email, chat, or other platforms — including company-approved tools and personal channels when the conduct relates to work
- Creating or sharing content that demeans or sexualizes a colleague

**Sexual Harassment Specifically:**

Sexual harassment is a form of sex discrimination and is illegal under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act and applicable state laws. It includes two types:

- **Quid pro quo:** When submission to or rejection of sexual conduct is made a condition of employment decisions (e.g., promotion, assignment, continued employment)
- **Hostile work environment:** When sexual conduct is sufficiently severe or pervasive to create a work environment that a reasonable person would find hostile, intimidating, or abusive

### 2.2 Zero-Tolerance Standard

The organization operates under a zero-tolerance policy for harassment. This means:

- All reported incidents will be investigated, regardless of the seniority of the alleged perpetrator
- Substantiated harassment will result in disciplinary action, which may include immediate termination
- Retaliation against anyone who reports harassment, participates in an investigation, or opposes harassment is strictly prohibited and will itself be treated as a serious violation of this Code
- Harassers will not be protected on the basis of past performance, tenure, or business value to the company

### 2.3 Harassment in Remote and Hybrid Environments

Harassment does not require physical proximity. Conduct that occurs through email, Slack, video conferencing, social media, or any other digital channel is subject to the same standards as in-person conduct. Inappropriate behavior during virtual meetings — including unwanted screen sharing of offensive content, making demeaning comments in chat, or recording meetings without consent — constitutes harassment and will be treated accordingly.

## 3. Conflict of Interest Policy

### 3.1 What Is a Conflict of Interest?

A conflict of interest arises when an employee's personal, financial, or outside professional interests interfere — or could reasonably appear to interfere — with their ability to act in the best interests of the organization. Conflicts of interest can be direct (you personally benefit from a business decision you influence) or indirect (a family member or close associate benefits).

The existence of a potential conflict is not automatically disqualifying. Many conflicts can be managed effectively through disclosure and appropriate recusal. What is unacceptable is a conflict that is not disclosed.

### 3.2 Common Conflict of Interest Scenarios

**Outside Employment and Business Activities:**

Employees may engage in outside employment, consulting, or business activities only if those activities do not conflict with their duties to the company, do not use company resources, and do not compete with the company's business interests. Prior written approval from your manager and HR is required before accepting any outside employment.

**Financial Interests:**

Employees with purchasing, procurement, or vendor management responsibilities must not hold a material financial interest in any company from which the organization procures goods or services. A "material financial interest" generally means ownership of more than 1% of a publicly traded company, or any ownership stake in a privately held company.

**Family and Personal Relationships:**

Employees must not participate in hiring decisions, performance evaluations, compensation decisions, or contract negotiations involving an immediate family member or romantic partner. If such a situation arises, the employee must disclose the relationship and recuse themselves from the relevant decision.

**Board Memberships and Affiliations:**

Serving on the board of directors of any for-profit organization requires advance disclosure to the Legal team. Serving on the board of a competitor is prohibited.

### 3.3 Disclosure Requirements

Employees who identify a real or potential conflict of interest must disclose it in writing to their manager and HR within 10 business days of becoming aware of it. Disclosures are reviewed confidentially. The company will work with the employee to determine appropriate management steps, which may include recusal, divestiture, or — in rare cases — role reassignment.

Failure to disclose a known conflict of interest is itself a serious violation of this Code.

## 4. Gift-Giving and Entertainment Policy

### 4.1 The $50 Maximum Rule

To protect both employees and the organization from any appearance of impropriety, all gifts — whether given to or received from clients, vendors, partners, or other third parties — are subject to a strict $50 per-person per-year limit.

**Gifts received from third parties:**
- Gifts valued at $50 or less may generally be accepted if they are infrequent and not intended to influence a business decision
- Gifts exceeding $50 in value must be declined or, if declining is not practicable (e.g., a perishable item), reported to HR immediately so that the item can be donated or redistributed
- Cash or cash equivalents (gift cards, prepaid debit cards) may not be accepted in any amount

**Gifts given to third parties:**
- Gifts given to clients, vendors, or partners on behalf of the company must be approved in advance and may not exceed $50 per recipient per year
- All company-funded gifts must be recorded in the expense system with a clear description of the business purpose

### 4.2 Business Entertainment

Business meals, events, and entertainment involving clients or vendors are permitted when they serve a legitimate business purpose and are reasonable in scope. "Reasonable" means it would not embarrass the company if publicly disclosed and would not appear to be an inducement for favorable treatment.

Entertainment expenses must be submitted through the standard expense reporting process with clear documentation of the attendees and the business purpose. Unusually lavish entertainment — regardless of the dollar amount — may be flagged for review.

### 4.3 Anti-Bribery Compliance

The organization complies fully with the U.S. Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) and applicable anti-bribery laws in all jurisdictions where it operates. Payments, gifts, or anything of value given to any government official — foreign or domestic — for the purpose of obtaining or retaining business are strictly prohibited. This prohibition applies regardless of whether the payment is made directly by an employee or indirectly through a third party. Questions about FCPA compliance should be directed to Legal immediately.

## 5. Workplace Violence Prevention

The organization is committed to maintaining a workplace free from violence, threats, and intimidation. Any conduct that threatens the safety or well-being of any person on company premises or at company-sponsored events is strictly prohibited. This includes:

- Physical altercations or threats of physical harm
- Destruction of property
- Possession of weapons on company property, regardless of whether the employee holds a permit
- Intimidating or menacing behavior, online or in person

Employees who witness or experience any threatening behavior should contact HR or Security immediately. If there is an immediate threat to physical safety, call 911 first.

## 6. Reporting Concerns and Non-Retaliation

### 6.1 How to Report

The organization provides multiple channels for raising concerns, reporting potential violations, or seeking guidance. You are encouraged to use whichever channel feels most appropriate to your situation:

**Direct Reporting:**
- Your direct manager or HR Business Partner (for non-sensitive concerns)
- The Legal & Compliance team (for legal or regulatory concerns)
- The CISO or IT Security team (for technology-related concerns)

**Anonymous Reporting:**
- **Ethics & Compliance Hotline:** 1-800-XXX-XXXX (available 24/7, operated by an independent third party)
- **Online Reporting Portal:** ethics.company.com (anonymous submissions accepted)

You may choose to remain anonymous when using the hotline or online portal. While anonymous reports make it harder to investigate thoroughly, the company takes all reports seriously and will take action where the facts permit.

### 6.2 Non-Retaliation Policy

Retaliation against any employee who, in good faith, reports a potential violation of this Code, participates in an investigation, or refuses to participate in conduct that violates this Code is strictly prohibited. Retaliation includes adverse employment actions (demotion, termination, reduction in hours) as well as informal mistreatment (ostracism, increased scrutiny, exclusion from meetings).

If you believe you have been retaliated against for reporting a concern, report it immediately through the same channels listed above. Retaliation is itself a serious violation of this Code and will be investigated and addressed with the same rigor as the underlying complaint.

## 7. Disciplinary Process

Violations of this Code are subject to a range of disciplinary responses depending on the severity, frequency, and circumstances of the conduct. Possible outcomes include:

- Verbal or written warning
- Mandatory training or counseling
- Performance improvement plan (PIP)
- Suspension with or without pay
- Demotion or reassignment
- Termination of employment

The company reserves the right to bypass progressive discipline and move directly to termination for serious violations, including harassment, fraud, violence, or conduct that poses an immediate risk to others.
