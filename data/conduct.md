# Conduct Policy: Workplace Standards and Professionalism

**Document Type:** Workplace Conduct Policy
**Effective Date:** January 1, 2025
**Applies To:** All Employees, Contractors, Temporary Workers, and Board Members
**Owner:** Human Resources / Legal & Compliance
**Review Cycle:** Annual
**Classification:** Internal Use

---

## 1. Purpose and Core Values

This Conduct Policy defines the standards of professional behavior expected of everyone who works for or with this organization. It reflects our commitment to building a workplace where all individuals are treated with dignity and respect, where ethical behavior is the norm rather than the exception, and where the organization's reputation and resources are protected through individual accountability.

Compliance with this policy is not optional. All employees, contractors, and board members are required to understand and adhere to these standards as a condition of their relationship with the organization. Ignorance of the policy is not an excuse for non-compliance. When situations arise that are not explicitly addressed here, employees are expected to apply the standard of "would I be comfortable if my manager, a colleague, or a client saw this?" and act accordingly.

Questions about how this policy applies to a specific situation should be directed to HR or Legal & Compliance before the fact, not after.

## 2. Workplace Harassment and Discrimination — Zero Tolerance

### 2.1 Protected Characteristics

The organization maintains an unwavering commitment to a workplace free from harassment, discrimination, and retaliation based on any protected characteristic, including: race, color, national origin, religion, sex, gender identity, gender expression, sexual orientation, age (40+), disability, pregnancy, marital status, veteran or military status, or any other characteristic protected under federal, state, or local law.

This commitment extends to all aspects of employment: hiring, compensation, assignment, promotion, training, discipline, and termination.

### 2.2 What Constitutes Harassment

Harassment is any unwelcome conduct — verbal, physical, written, visual, or digital — based on a protected characteristic that creates a hostile, intimidating, or offensive work environment, or that is made a condition of employment or employment decisions (quid pro quo harassment).

Examples of prohibited conduct include: derogatory jokes or slurs; unwanted physical contact; persistent romantic or sexual advances after the recipient has expressed discomfort or disinterest; distribution of offensive materials; public humiliation; threats; and any conduct that a reasonable person would find hostile or abusive given the totality of circumstances.

Digital and remote conduct is subject to the same standards as in-person conduct. Harassment via email, chat, social media, or video conference is treated with equal seriousness.

### 2.3 Zero Tolerance and Mandatory Investigation

The company's zero-tolerance standard means:

Every report of harassment or discrimination will be promptly and thoroughly investigated, regardless of the parties' seniority, role, or tenure. Substantiated findings will result in disciplinary action up to and including immediate termination. No prior performance record, business value, or personal relationship will mitigate consequences for confirmed harassment. The company will not require a victim to choose between continued exposure to harassment and an adverse employment action. Retaliation against any person who reports harassment, participates in an investigation, or refuses to tolerate harassing conduct is itself a serious violation of this policy and grounds for termination.

All reports are investigated with as much confidentiality as the investigative process allows. Complete anonymity cannot be guaranteed when investigating specific incidents, but information is shared only on a need-to-know basis.

## 3. Conflict of Interest Reporting

### 3.1 Definition and Scope

A conflict of interest exists when an employee's personal, financial, or external professional interests have the potential — actual or perceived — to interfere with their ability to make objective decisions in the company's best interest. Conflicts do not require proof of improper behavior; the appearance of a conflict is itself a governance concern that must be managed.

### 3.2 Situations Requiring Disclosure

Employees must disclose the following in writing to HR within 10 business days of becoming aware of the situation:

**Personal Financial Interests:** Ownership of more than 1% of any publicly traded company that the organization does business with, or any ownership stake in a private company that is a vendor, client, or competitor.

**Outside Employment:** Any paid work, consulting engagement, board seat, or advisory role at an outside organization — whether or not it appears to compete with the company's business.

**Family and Romantic Relationships:** Any situation where an employee is in a supervisory, financial, or decision-making role relative to a family member or romantic partner. This includes hiring, performance evaluation, compensation decisions, and contract awards.

**Gifts and Benefits from Third Parties:** Any gift, benefit, or hospitality received from a vendor, client, or business partner that exceeds the limits described in Section 4 of this policy.

### 3.3 Disclosure Process and Resolution

Disclosures are submitted via the Conflict of Interest Disclosure Form on the HR portal. HR will review the disclosure in consultation with Legal and determine the appropriate resolution, which may include recusal from specific decisions, divestiture of a financial interest, reassignment of reporting relationships, or — in rare cases — role reassignment. Employees are not penalized for good-faith disclosure. Employees who fail to disclose a known conflict may face disciplinary action independent of whether any improper conduct occurred.

## 4. Gift-Giving and Receiving Policy

### 4.1 The $50 Limit

To prevent even the appearance of impropriety or undue influence in business relationships, the company applies a strict limit of **$50 USD per person per calendar year** on gifts — given or received — in connection with company business.

**Gifts received from external parties:** Gifts valued at $50 or less may be accepted when they are infrequent, unsolicited, and not intended to influence a business decision. Gifts exceeding $50 must be politely declined. If declining is not practicable (e.g., a gift is sent to your home address), notify HR immediately. The item will be returned, donated, or redistributed based on its nature.

**Cash or cash equivalents:** Gift cards, prepaid debit cards, checks, and cash may not be accepted in any amount. This is a firm prohibition with no exceptions.

**Gifts given on behalf of the company:** Gifts to clients, vendors, or partners must be pre-approved by the employee's manager and may not exceed $50 per recipient per year. All company-funded gifts must be recorded in the expense system with a business purpose notation.

### 4.2 Prohibited Gifts

The following are prohibited regardless of value: gifts from or to parties involved in pending contract negotiations, government officials (domestic or foreign, due to FCPA implications), or any party where acceptance could influence an ongoing procurement or audit process.

## 5. Dress Code

### 5.1 Client-Facing Roles

Employees in client-facing roles — including sales, account management, consulting delivery, and executive functions — are expected to dress at or above the client's standard of professional dress when in client environments. As a baseline, business professional attire is required for formal client meetings, presentations, and events: tailored suits, dress shirts, blouses, dress slacks, and formal footwear.

When the expected client environment is more casual (e.g., a tech company's casual culture), employees may dress accordingly — but should always err toward slightly more polished than the client's average employee, not less.

For video meetings with clients, professional attire from the waist up is required. Ensure your background (physical or virtual) is clean and professional.

### 5.2 Internal and Hybrid Roles

Employees whose work is primarily internal may dress in business casual attire on regular workdays: neat, clean clothing without offensive graphics, slogans, or visible damage. Examples of acceptable business casual include chinos, blouses, polo shirts, loafers, and clean sneakers.

**Clothing that is not acceptable in any work context:**
- Clothing with offensive, discriminatory, or sexually explicit text or graphics
- Clothing that reveals undergarments or is excessively revealing
- Apparel associated with political or partisan advocacy in client-facing or cross-functional settings

### 5.3 Remote and Home Office Dress

Employees working from home are expected to dress appropriately for video meetings with colleagues, clients, and partners. Business casual is the minimum standard for scheduled video calls. Attending internal video calls in sleepwear is not appropriate professional conduct.

## 6. Use of Company Property for Personal Gain

Company property — including physical equipment, intellectual property, software licenses, office supplies, company data, brand assets, mailing lists, and proprietary business information — may not be used for personal benefit, outside business activities, or any purpose other than authorized company business.

Specific prohibitions include: using the company's mailing list or client database to solicit business for a personal venture; using company-licensed software for personal freelance projects; using the company's brand or logo to represent a personal business or affiliate relationship; using office supplies or equipment for personal projects without authorization; and using the company's intellectual property — including processes, templates, or proprietary methodologies — in work performed for outside clients.

Misuse of company property may result in disciplinary action and may also constitute grounds for civil or criminal legal action, particularly where it involves misappropriation of trade secrets or intellectual property.

## 7. Disciplinary Action Ladder

The organization uses a structured disciplinary process that is progressive in most circumstances, allowing employees to correct conduct issues at each stage before more serious consequences apply. However, the company reserves the right to bypass any step in this ladder and proceed directly to termination for conduct that is sufficiently serious.

### Step 1: Verbal Warning

For minor, first-time violations of workplace policies or professional conduct standards, the manager delivers a private verbal warning. The conversation documents the specific behavior, the policy violated, the expected change, and a timeline for improvement. A written record of the verbal warning is placed in the employee's HR file.

### Step 2: Written Warning

If the behavior continues after a verbal warning, or if the initial violation is more serious, a formal written warning is issued. The written warning describes the conduct issue in detail, references prior verbal counseling, states the consequences of continued non-compliance, and may include an action plan or performance improvement plan (PIP) with measurable expectations and a review date.

### Step 3: Final Written Warning / Suspension

A third violation — or a second serious violation — results in a final written warning, which may be accompanied by a suspension without pay. Suspension periods are typically 1 to 5 business days. The final written warning makes explicit that the next violation of policy or failure to meet the action plan will result in termination.

### Step 4: Termination

Continued policy violations following a final written warning, or a single incident of sufficiently serious misconduct, result in termination of employment. Termination decisions are made by the employee's manager in consultation with HR and, for senior roles, with Legal and executive leadership.

**Grounds for immediate termination without prior steps include:** confirmed harassment or discrimination, workplace violence or threats, fraud or theft, willful destruction of company property, gross insubordination, unauthorized disclosure of trade secrets or confidential information, and serious violations of legal or regulatory obligations.
