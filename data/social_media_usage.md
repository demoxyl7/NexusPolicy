# Social Media Usage Policy: Brand Safety

**Document Type:** Social Media and Online Communications Policy  
**Effective Date:** January 1, 2025  
**Applies To:** All Employees, Contractors, and Vendors Acting on Behalf of the Company  
**Owner:** Marketing / Legal & Compliance / Human Resources  
**Review Cycle:** Annual  
**Classification:** Internal Use

---

## 1. Purpose and Scope

Social media has fundamentally changed how individuals, organizations, and brands communicate. It has created extraordinary opportunities for connection, creativity, and community — and equally significant risks for organizations whose employees operate online at the intersection of their personal and professional lives.

This policy exists to help you navigate that intersection thoughtfully. It is not designed to silence employees or prevent you from having an authentic online presence. On the contrary, we recognize that employees who are knowledgeable advocates for our brand — and who feel empowered to engage professionally online — create real business value. Our goal is to give you the guidance you need to be confident in how you show up online, while protecting both you and the organization from avoidable harm.

This policy applies to all social media activity that relates to or could reasonably be perceived as relating to the company, its clients, its employees, its competitors, or its industry — whether that activity occurs on company-owned accounts or your personal profiles. It applies during work hours and outside of them.

Platforms in scope include, but are not limited to: LinkedIn, X (formerly Twitter), Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, YouTube, Reddit, Threads, WhatsApp groups, Discord servers, Substack, personal blogs, and any other public-facing digital communication channel.

## 2. Personal Accounts vs. Official Company Accounts

Understanding the distinction between personal and professional social media activity is foundational to this policy.

### 2.1 Official Company Accounts

Official company social media accounts are accounts that have been established, verified, and are managed on behalf of the organization by authorized employees. These accounts include channels operated by the Marketing, Communications, and PR teams, as well as any team-specific or product-specific accounts approved by leadership.

**You may not:**
- Create new social media accounts in the company's name or using the company's branding, logo, or trademarks without explicit written authorization from the Marketing and Legal teams
- Post on official company accounts unless you have been designated as an authorized administrator or content contributor for that account
- Use the company's name, logo, or visual identity in personal account headers, bios, or profile photos in a way that implies you speak on behalf of the organization

**Authorized account administrators must:**
- Follow all content approval workflows defined by Marketing
- Escalate any sensitive, legally complex, or unexpected content situations to the Communications team before posting
- Report unauthorized access to any company account immediately to both IT Security and Marketing

### 2.2 Personal Accounts: Your Voice, Your Responsibility

You are free to maintain personal social media accounts and to post content that reflects your own opinions, interests, and experiences. Personal expression is a right we respect. However, when your personal activity touches on the company — including its people, products, competitors, clients, or industry — additional standards apply.

**When posting about work-related topics from personal accounts:**

- Make it unmistakably clear that you are expressing your own views, not the company's. A standard disclaimer such as "Views are my own" in your bio or within the post itself is strongly recommended for employees who regularly post about professional topics
- Do not state or imply that your personal views are endorsed by or represent the position of the organization
- Do not post anything you would be uncomfortable having your manager, the CEO, or a client read
- Use good judgment about timing and context — a casual LinkedIn comment about industry trends is different from a public statement about a client relationship, an internal conflict, or a regulatory development affecting the company

## 3. What You May and May Not Share Online

### 3.1 Permitted Content

You are encouraged to share:

- Your professional accomplishments, career milestones, and personal growth (e.g., certifications earned, projects you are proud of)
- Publicly available information about the company that has been released through official channels (press releases, published blog posts, product announcements)
- Thoughtful commentary on industry trends, professional skills, and leadership topics
- Pride in your work and your team, without disclosing confidential details
- Engagement with the company's official posts (liking, sharing, commenting constructively)

### 3.2 Prohibited Content

The following content is strictly prohibited on any social media channel, whether personal or professional:

**Confidential and Proprietary Information:**  
You may not share any information that is classified as Confidential or Restricted under the company's Information Classification Policy. This includes — but is not limited to — unreleased product plans or roadmaps, financial data not yet publicly filed, client names or details (unless the client has publicly disclosed the relationship), internal strategy documents, personnel matters, litigation-related information, or any information shared in confidence by a client or partner.

**Client and Partner Confidentiality:**  
Discussions about clients — even in general terms that could enable identification — are prohibited without explicit written authorization from the client and approval from the Legal team. Do not post screenshots of client communications, deliverables, or meetings.

**Disparagement:**  
Do not post content that disparages clients, competitors, partners, colleagues, or the company itself. Criticism of the organization's strategy, culture, or leadership should be raised through internal channels (see Section 6), not aired publicly. Disparaging social media posts can expose you to personal liability and constitute grounds for termination.

**Discriminatory, Harassing, or Threatening Content:**  
Any content that would violate the company's Code of Conduct — including content that is harassing, discriminatory, threatening, or sexually explicit — is prohibited, even on personal accounts when directed at colleagues, clients, or others in your professional network.

## 4. NDA and Confidentiality Reminders

### 4.1 Your Confidentiality Obligations Extend Beyond Employment

Most employees sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) as a condition of employment. Your confidentiality obligations under that agreement do not expire when you clock out — and in many cases, they survive the termination of your employment. If you are unsure whether information you want to share is covered by your NDA, the answer is almost certainly: do not share it until you have confirmed with Legal.

### 4.2 What Counts as Confidential Under Your NDA

Your NDA broadly covers any information disclosed to you in the course of your employment that is not already publicly available and that the company treats as confidential. This includes:

- Business strategies, plans, and financial projections
- Customer lists, CRM data, and client communications
- Technical specifications, source code, and product roadmaps
- Personnel information about colleagues (compensation, performance, personal circumstances)
- Vendor agreements, pricing arrangements, and negotiation strategies
- Any information shared in meetings, documents, or conversations marked "confidential" or "internal only"

### 4.3 Social Media as a NDA Risk Vector

Employees sometimes inadvertently breach their NDA on social media — not through deliberate disclosure, but through context clues, screenshots, casual references, or geotagging. A seemingly innocuous post that combines a photo at an unannounced client site with a comment about an exciting new project can constitute a breach. Think before you post.

If you believe you have accidentally disclosed confidential information on a social media platform, take the following steps immediately:

1. Delete the post
2. Report the incident to your manager and the Legal & Compliance team
3. Document what was shared and with whom

Early disclosure of accidental breaches is treated much more favorably than discovered breaches.

## 5. Representing the Brand Professionally

### 5.1 You Are Always an Ambassador

Whether or not you intend it, your public online presence reflects on the company. Clients, candidates, partners, investors, and journalists will inevitably find your professional profile and may associate your conduct with our brand. This doesn't mean you need to curate a sanitized, corporate persona — it means applying the same standard of professionalism online that you bring to the office.

### 5.2 Dos and Don'ts for Brand-Safe Social Media Presence

**Do:**
- Keep your LinkedIn profile accurate and up to date — it is a direct reflection of our talent brand
- Share company content through official sharing features rather than screenshotting and reposting
- Engage positively and professionally in comments and replies
- Use appropriate language and tone — what's appropriate with friends may not be appropriate on a professional network
- Proactively flag positive media coverage or industry recognition to the Marketing team for amplification

**Don't:**
- Make statements about the company's financial condition, strategy, or future plans
- Comment publicly on regulatory, legal, or government matters affecting the company without authorization from Legal
- Engage in public arguments with critics of the company, clients, or competitors — escalate to Communications instead
- Post about internal conflicts, layoffs, restructuring, or HR matters
- Photograph or film internal meetings, workspaces, or events without prior approval

## 6. Crisis Communication Protocol

### 6.1 What Constitutes a Social Media Crisis

A social media crisis is any event in which online commentary could cause significant reputational, legal, or operational harm to the organization. Examples include: a viral negative post about the company from a customer or employee, data breach disclosure, regulatory action, a public controversy involving an employee, or breaking news coverage that mischaracterizes the company's practices.

### 6.2 Do Not Respond Unilaterally

In a crisis situation, it is critical that all public communication flows through a single, authorized voice — typically the Communications or PR team, in coordination with Legal and Executive Leadership. Individual employees who post personal responses to crisis events — even with the best intentions — can escalate the situation, create legal liability, or contradict the official company position.

**If you become aware of an emerging social media crisis:**

1. Do not post any response, comment, or reaction relating to the situation on any platform
2. Do not share the viral content from your personal accounts
3. Do not speculate about the facts of the situation with colleagues on public channels
4. Alert the Communications team immediately: comms@company.com or the designated crisis line
5. Await guidance before taking any public action

### 6.3 Monitoring and Escalation

The Marketing and Communications team actively monitors the organization's social media presence using listening tools. If you observe concerning content — including negative trending coverage, inflammatory posts by former employees, or emerging customer complaints going viral — flag it to the team immediately rather than waiting for them to discover it independently.

## 7. Consequences of Policy Violations

Violations of this Social Media Policy may result in disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment. The severity of the response will depend on the nature, intent, and impact of the violation. Legal action may also be pursued in cases involving breach of NDA, defamation, or unauthorized disclosure of regulated information.
